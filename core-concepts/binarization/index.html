Core Concepts

Binarization

Binarization

Binarization is the process of converting an image into a binary that consists of two pixel classes.

For our manuscripts, foreground is ink that is text or other meaningful marks. Background is paper, stains, shadows, scan texture, etc.

Why binarization is difficult

Real images contain uneven lighting, paper texture, ink bleed, weak strokes, shadows, stains, and sensor noise. A binarization algorithm may therefore:

  • miss faint foreground
  • turn stains into false foreground
  • break thin strokes
  • erase dots, punctuation, or diacritics
  • keep speckles as text

The task is to correct these mistakes manually.

Binarized draft looks good to me, why not use that?

The binarized draft has already been processed by an algorithm and noise filter. It may be close to correct, but it can't be a ground truth. See Binarized Draft for the longer answer.

How do you evaluate binarization?

Once ground truths are created, binarization algorithms' outputs can be compared pixel by pixel against the ground truth.
